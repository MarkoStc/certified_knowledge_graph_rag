# Certified selective prediction (P10 / H3)

Generated by `scripts/make_selective_report.py`. Under the RAG-Safety
attack, a selective predictor answers its most-trusted queries and
abstains on the rest. We compare two abstention signals by **AURC** (area
under the risk-coverage curve, lower = better): the certificate `k` vs
self-consistency (agreement across sampled generations).

H3: `k` is the better signal — a model can be *consistently wrong* through
a forged single chain (k=0) that self-consistency rates confident.

| dataset | model | n | attacked acc | AURC k | AURC self-consist. | winner |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| metaqa-2hop | Qwen2.5-7B | 613 | 0.61 | 0.305 | 0.374 | **k** |
| metaqa-3hop | Qwen2.5-7B | 1394 | 0.73 | 0.236 | 0.277 | **k** |

## metaqa-2hop risk at coverage

| coverage | risk (k signal) | risk (self-consistency) |
|---|---:|---:|
| 25% | 0.28 | n/a* |
| 50% | 0.29 | n/a* |
| 75% | 0.30 | n/a* |
| 100% | 0.39 | 0.39 |

\* n/a = the signal is too coarse to operate at that coverage (large tie-groups). Self-consistency lumps most queries into 'fully agree', so it cannot abstain finely — itself a point in `k`'s favour; the AURC above integrates the full curve regardless.

## Takeaway

Lower AURC for `k` means abstaining on low-`k` queries removes the
attack-vulnerable ones more effectively than abstaining on low-confidence
ones — the certificate is grounded in the graph's redundancy, which is
exactly what the insertion attack exploits, whereas self-consistency can
be high on a confidently-forged answer (AGENTS.md P10 / H3).
